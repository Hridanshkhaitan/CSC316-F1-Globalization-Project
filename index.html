F1'S RAPID EXPANSION

FROM EUROPEAN ROOTS TO GLOBAL SPECTACLE

F1 used to be primarily European with 62% of races in 2016 being held in Europe. In 2017, Liberty Media purchased Formula 1 for $4.4 billion, transforming F1 into a global entertainment powerhouse. The new ownership implemented an aggressive expansion strategy targeting key markets like USA, Middle East, and Asia. Now, Europe only hosts 41% of the F1 races as of 2024.

THE PRICE OF POPULARITY

Ticket prices have skyrocketed – the average weekend ticket increased by 82% globally since 2017. Traditional European races with rich history (like Spa-Francorchamps and Monza) face uncertain futures due to financial pressures. While global accessibility has improved through digital media, the authentic regional character of the sport risks being homogenized.

But what sparked this dramatic shift in Formula 1's global strategy? A perfect storm of media innovation, changing demographics, and one particularly successful Netflix documentary series changed everything...

The Victory in Vegas

AN ECONOMIC SUCCESS

F1's opening of Las Vegas saw an economic impact of 1.5 billion $ stimulating the local economy and bringing in more tourists. In fact, Vegas' impact greatly exceeded that of any other race in recent years. Indeed, the impact of the Vegas GP measured in dollars is over 32 times larger than certain other races. This indicates that cities and countries may benefit from hosting an F1 race, further aiding in the Globalization efforts.

THE FINANCIAL INCENTIVE

Liberty Media aquired F1 in 2017. Under new management the sport has seen slow yet steady increase in revenue year after year. However, the reintroduction of the Las Vegas GP saw record breaking revenue in 2023 of 3.2 billion $. This represents the first time the F1 group had revenue exceeding the 3 billion mark. The fact that F1 revenue first exceeded $3 billion in 2023, driven in part by the success of international events like the Las Vegas Grand Prix, clearly showcases the financial incentive for globalizing the sport.

But what has this global expansion meant for Formula 1’s most loyal fans? For many longtime supporters in Europe and other traditional markets, the sport’s rapid globalization has sparked a growing sense of alienation — as if the heart of F1 is being reshaped for newer, flashier audiences far from its historic roots...
